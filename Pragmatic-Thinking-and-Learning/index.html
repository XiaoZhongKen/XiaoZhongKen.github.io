1. Intro

two most important skills author thinks are:

  • Learning and Thinking

Programmers have to learn constantly—not just the stereotypical new technologies but also the problem domain of the application, the whims of the user community, the quirks of their teammates, the shifting sands of the industry, and the evolving characteristics of the project itself as it is built. We have to learn—and relearn— constantly. Then we have to apply this learning to the daily barrage of both old and new problems.
It sounds easy enough in principle perhaps, but learning, criti- cal thinking, creativity, and invention—all those mind-expanding skills—are all up to you. You don’t get taught; you have to learn. We tend to look at the teacher/learner relationship the wrong way around: it’s not that the teacher teaches; it’s that the student learns. The learning is always up to you.

In systems thinking, one tries to envision an object as a connection point of several systems,
rather than as a discrete object unto itself.

2. Journey from Novice to Expert

The Five Dreyfus Model Stages

  • Stage 5: Expert
    Experts are the primary sources of knowlege and information in any field.
    Experts work from intuition, not from reason.
  • Stage 4: Proficient
    seek out and want to understand the larger conceptual framework around this skill. can take full advantage of the reflection and feedback that is the core agile methods.
  • Stage 3: Competent
    can now develop conceptual models of the problem domain and work with
    those models effectively. They can troubleshoot problems on their own and begin to figure out how to solve novel problems—ones they haven’t faced before.
    can’t apply agile methods the way we would like—there isn’t yet enough
    ability for reflection and self-correction
  • Stage 2: Advanced Beginers
    They can try tasks on their own, but they still have difficulty troubleshooting.
    no holistic understanding and really don’t wan’t it yet
  • Stage 1: Novices
    context free rules to follow

5. Debug Your Mind

5.1 Meet Your Cognitive Biases

Anchoring
思维锚定

Fundamental attribution error
将表象认为是本质，忽略上下文

Remember that behavior is oftentimes more because of reaction to a context than because of fundamental personality traits.

Self-serving bias
自我服务偏向—将成功归因为自我因素, 把失败归因为外部因素的倾向

Need for colosure

We are not comfortable with doubt and uncertainty—so much so that we’ll go to great lengths to resolve open issues and to remove uncertainty and reach closure.

Confirmation bias

Everyone looks for choice facts to fit your own preconceptions and pet theories.

Hawthore effect

a tendency to change their behaviors when they know they are being studied.

False memory

every read is a write. Your memories are constantly rewritten in light of your current context

Symbolic reduction fallacy

which loses at least the nuances and sometimes even the truth of the matter.

Nominal fallacy

naming alone does not offer any useful understanding.

Lizard Logic

  • Fight, flight, or fright
    战斗，逃跑，惊吓
  • Get it now
    即刻满足
  • Be dominant
    霸道
  • Defend the territory
    固守领地，needy
  • If it hurts, hiss
    抱怨，而不是解决问题
  • Like me == good; not like me == bad
    顺我者昌，逆我者亡

Monkey See, Monkey Do
优秀会传染
坏情绪也会传染

Acting Evolved
进化过的行为

These lizardlike behaviors are inherent in the wiring, not in the higher-level cognitive thought processes. Thinking takes time; these actions and reactions work more quickly than that, and with less effort.

Breathe out, deeply, and get rid of the stale air. Breathe in, deeply. Count to ten. Remember that you’re the evolved one. Let the lizard reaction pass, and allow the neocortex to process the event.

The mind is not a vessel to be filled, but a fire to be kindled.

Education comes from the Latin word educare, which literally means “led out,”

The model you build in your mind, the questions you ask to build that model, and your experiences and practices built up along the way and that you use daily are far more relevant to your performance

6.3 Create a Pragmatic Investment Plan

  • Have a concrete plan.

    use the idea of SMART objectives and goals, and devise dif- ferent levels of goals over time. For instance:

    • Now (what’s the next action you can take)
    • Goals for next year
    • Goals for five years out

    the planning is far more important than the plan. The plan will change, as we’ll see next. But getting in tune with your goals is invaluable.

  • Diversify.

    Part of diversification is considering the risk vs. return ratio.

  • Make an active, not passive, investment.

    you want to always evaluate your plan in the cold light of day and realistically judge how it’s going.

    Perhaps it’s time to add a few new elements that you hadn’t consid- ered previously or scrap a few plans that just aren’t working out.

  • Make a regular investment.

6.7 Read Deliberately with SQ3R

reading is the least effective means of learning, compared to any sort of experiential learning.

it seems that we learn best from observation. We are natural mimics, and the best, most effective way to learn is by observing and mimicking someone else

  • Survey: Scan the table of contents and chapter summaries for an overview.
  • Question: Note any questions you have.
  • Read: Read in its entirety.
  • Recite: Summarize, take notes, and put in your own words.
  • Review: Reread, expand notes, and discuss with colleagues.

Test-Driven Learning

  • Repeated input, by itself, doesn’t do you nearly as much good.
  • Repeatedly testing yourself by trying to recall the material over and over works much better. Deliberate, repeated attempts at retrieval consolidate learning and strengthen the connections in your brain.

spacing effect

  • Cramming, or studying a lot of information in a short amount of time, is not very effective. We tend to forget things along an exponential curve
  • spacing out your quizzing reinforces material much more effectively. For example, you might plan on retesting yourself along a 2-2- 2-6 schedule: retest after two hours, two days, two weeks, and six months.

8.5 Manage Interruptions Deliberately

Can’t All Be Winners

Not every day will be a productive day. If events turn chaotic, it might be more effective to acknowledge that and realize you’re just not going to get in the groove. Fight the fires, enjoy the pizza at your desk, and hope for a better tomorrow.

The more you can keep information in context, the better. Person- ally, this is why I tend to have a lot of piles on or near my office desk. I call that context. The cleaners call it “a mess.”

But often “out of sight” means “out of mind.”
